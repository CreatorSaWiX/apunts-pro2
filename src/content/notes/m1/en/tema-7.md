## 0. Geometric Transformations

 > **Do you want to program GTA VII?** 
 > If your dream is to work at Rockstar Games or create the next revolutionary graphics engine, this is the most important topic of your entire degree. Camera rotations, character movement and object physics are, in essence, **Linear Maps**. Also, put some effort into it: this topic usually represents **40% of the final exam**.

In $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$ exercises, the most typical transformations you'll see (and that shaders in any video game use) are:

*   **Rotation**: Turning objects around an axis.
*   **Reflection**: The mirror effect (change of orientation).
*   **Projection**: "Flattening" objects onto a plane or axis (fundamental for rendering 3D on 2D screens).
*   **Scaling**: Making things bigger or smaller.

::three{type="vis_transformacions_hibrida"}

> **3D Geometry**: While in $\mathbb{R}^2$ we only have one axis of rotation, in $\mathbb{R}^3$ we can rotate around $X, Y$ or $Z$. Notice how **reflection** inverts the object; it's a key concept in computer graphics.

## 1. What is a Linear Map?

A **linear map** is a function between two vector spaces (let's say $E$ and $F$) that "respects" the structure of these spaces. It's not just any function; it's a "straight" and "proportional" transformation.

For a function $f: E \to F$ to be linear, it must meet **two sacred conditions**:

1.  **Addition**: $f(\vec{u} + \vec{v}) = f(\vec{u}) + f(\vec{v})$  
    *(Transforming the sum is the same as summing the transformations).*
2.  **Scalar Multiplication**: $f(\lambda \cdot \vec{u}) = \lambda \cdot f(\vec{u})$  
    *(If you double the input, the output is doubled).*

A linear map **always** sends the zero vector to the zero vector ($f(\vec{0}_E) = \vec{0}_F$). If you see a function where $f(0,0) = (1,2)$, you know for sure that it is **not** linear!

::mafs{type="vis_propietats_lineals"}

### How to work with polynomials and matrices?
When the exercise is not about $\mathbb{R}^n$, the first step is to convert the objects into "number vectors" (coordinates) with respect to a basis. Once you have the vectors, the exercise is solved exactly the same as in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
*   **Polynomials ($\mathbb{R}_n[x]$)**: A polynomial $ax^2 + bx + c$ becomes the vector $(a, b, c)$ if we use the basis $\{x^2, x, 1\}$.
*   **Matrices ($\mathcal{M}_{2 \times 2}$)**: A matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ becomes the vector $(a, b, c, d)$ using the canonical matrix basis.

## 2. The Associated Matrix

Working with formulas like $f(x,y) = (2x+y, x-y)$ is tiring. Luckily, all the information of a linear map can be stored in a **matrix**. This matrix acts as a "translator".

::mafs{type="vis_matriu_associada"}

### How is the matrix $M_W^B(f)$ built?
The recipe is always the same (and it's the typical exam question):

1.  **Take the vectors of the domain basis** $B = \{\vec{b}_1, \dots, \vec{b}_n\}$.
2.  **Calculate their image** applying the formula: $f(\vec{b}_1), f(\vec{b}_2), \dots$
3.  **Express the results in coordinates** of the codomain basis $W$.
4.  **Put the results as columns** in the matrix.

> **Images as columns**. If you remember this phrase, you have half the exam done. As you can see in the visualization above, moving the matrix values is literally moving where the images of the basis vectors point.

**The fundamental equation:**
$$[f(\vec{v})]_W = M_W^B(f) \cdot [\vec{v}]_B$$
This means: if you give me a vector in basis $B$ and I multiply it by the matrix, I get its image in basis $W$.

## 3. Where do the vectors go? Kernel and Image

This is the most important concept for solving dimension and subspace problems.

::three{type="vis_kernel_imatge_3d"}

### The Kernel (Ker f)
These are the vectors in the domain that "die" when going to zero.
*   **Definition**: $\text{Ker}(f) = \{ \vec{v} \in E : f(\vec{v}) = \vec{0} \}$
*   **Calculation**: You have to solve the homogeneous system $M \cdot \vec{x} = \vec{0}$.
*   **Interpretation**: If the Kernel only has the zero vector ($\text{Ker} = \{\vec{0}\}$), the map does not lose information. In the 3D lab above, the kernel is the red axis: any vector on this axis projects to the point $(0,0,0)$.

### The Image (Im f)
It is the subspace formed by all the vectors we "can reach" in the codomain.
*   **Calculation**: The Image is generated by the **columns** of the associated matrix.
*   **Image Basis**: If you put the matrix in row echelon form, the columns where there are "pivots" (chosen from the original matrix!) form a basis for the Image.
*   **Interpretation**: In the visualization, the image is the green plane. No matter where you move the blue vector, its image (green vector) will always be "trapped" within this plane.

### The Preimage (Going backwards)
The preimage of a vector $\vec{w}$, denoted as $f^{-1}(\vec{w})$, are all vectors $\vec{v}$ that when applying $f$ give $\vec{w}$.

::mafs{type="vis_antiimatge_subespais"}

*   **How is it calculated?** You must solve the **non-homogeneous** system $M \cdot \vec{x} = \vec{w}$.
*   **Result**: It can be a single vector, an entire subspace (if the system is indetermined consistent) or empty (if it is inconsistent).

### Transforming Subspaces (S)
If you have a subspace $S$ defined by its generators $S = \langle \vec{s}_1, \dots, \vec{s}_k \rangle$:
*   **Image of S**: $f(S) = \langle f(\vec{s}_1), \dots, f(\vec{s}_k) \rangle$. You just have to transform the generators.
*   **Preimage of S**: $f^{-1}(S)$ are the vectors that end up inside $S$. It is calculated by finding which $\vec{x}$ satisfy that $M \cdot \vec{x}$ is a linear combination of the basis of $S$.

### The Dimension Theorem (Rank-Nullity)
This formula will help you verify if your calculations are consistent:
$$\dim(\text{Domain}) = \dim(\text{Ker } f) + \dim(\text{Im } f)$$
*Hint: The dimension of the Image is the same as the **rank** of the matrix.*

## 4. Classification: What is our map like?

According to the dimensions of the Kernel and the Image, we classify the maps:

::mafs{type="vis_classificacio_aplicacions"}

*   **Injective (Monomorphism)**: No two different vectors go to the same place.
    *   Condition: $\text{Ker}(f) = \{\vec{0}\}$ (that is, $\dim \text{Ker} = 0$).
    *   Matrix Rank = $\dim(\text{Domain})$.
*   **Surjective (Epimorphism)**: The map "fills" the entire codomain.
    *   Condition: $\text{Im}(f) = F$ (that is, $\dim \text{Im} = \dim F$).
    *   Matrix Rank = $\dim(\text{Codomain})$.
*   **Bijective (Isomorphism)**: It is injective and surjective at the same time. It is a perfect 1 to 1 relationship.
    *   Condition: The matrix is square and its determinant is $\neq 0$.

> If the domain and codomain have the **same dimension**, then:
> Injective $\iff$ Surjective $\iff$ Bijective. If it meets one, it automatically meets them all!

### Special Case: Nilpotent Endomorphisms
An endomorphism is **nilpotent** if there is a number $k$ such that $f^k = \mathbf{0}$ (the zero map). 

::mafs{type="vis_endomorfisme_nilpotent"}

*   This means that if you apply the transformation several times in a row to any vector, you always end up reaching zero (**annihilation vortex**).
*   Its associated matrix has all eigenvalues equal to zero.

## 5. Composition and Inverse

### Composition ($g \circ f$)
If we apply first $f$ and then $g$, the composition matrix is the **product** of the matrices.
$$M(g \circ f) = M(g) \cdot M(f)$$

::mafs{type="vis_composicio_aplicacions"}

> **Order matters**: The order of the matrices is the reverse of the reading order. The one applied first ($f$) goes to the right of the product!

### Inverse ($f^{-1}$)
It only exists if $f$ is an isomorphism (bijective). The matrix of the inverse map is the inverse matrix of the original:
$$M(f^{-1}) = (M(f))^{-1}$$

::mafs{type="vis_inversa_aplicacio"}

## 6. Change of Basis: The "Sandwich Formula"

Sometimes they give us the matrix in the canonical bases ($C$), but we want it in other bases $B$ and $W$. The general formula that you must memorize is:
$$M_W^B(f) = P_{W \leftarrow C} \cdot M_C^C(f) \cdot P_{C \leftarrow B}$$

::mafs{type="vis_canvi_base_sandvitx"}

*   $P_{C \leftarrow B}$: Change of basis matrix (vectors of $B$ placed in columns).
*   $P_{W \leftarrow C}$: It is the **inverse** of the matrix that has the vectors of $W$ in columns ($P_{C \leftarrow W}^{-1}$).

**Visual logic**: To go from $B$ to $W$ through $f$, we take a "trip" in three stages:
1.  We jump from basis $B$ to the **canonical**.
2.  We apply transformation $f$ using the easy matrix (the canonical one).
3.  We jump from the canonical to the codomain basis **$W$**.
